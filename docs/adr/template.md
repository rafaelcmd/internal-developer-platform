## Context

What problem are we solving? What constraints exist? What forces are pushing
on the decision (cost, latency, team size, compliance, deadlines)? Anything
the reader needs to know to understand why this decision matters.

Keep this factual. Don't editorialize — that goes in **Decision** and
**Consequences**.

## Decision

What did we choose? State it in one or two sentences, then expand with the
specifics that future-you will need (concrete values, scope boundaries, what
the decision explicitly does *not* cover).

Use the active voice and present tense: "We use X" — not "We will use X" or
"X was chosen."

## Consequences

What becomes easier as a result of this decision? What becomes harder? What
did we give up?

Include both the positive and the negative. An ADR with no negative
consequences is usually one that hasn't been thought through.

## Alternatives considered

Each alternative gets a short paragraph: what it was, and why we ruled it out.
This is the most important section for future readers — it's what prevents
the same debate from being re-litigated in six months.

- **Alternative A** — why rejected.
- **Alternative B** — why rejected.

## When to revisit

Optional. Concrete signals that should trigger a fresh look at this decision
(e.g. "if duplicate publishes exceed 0.1% of traffic", "when we add a third
consumer service"). Saves the next person from inheriting a decision that
quietly went stale.
